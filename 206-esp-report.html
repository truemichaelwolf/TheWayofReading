The Development of English for Specific Purposes (ESP): A Scientific Report

Abstract

English for Specific Purposes (ESP) has evolved from a pragmatic response to post-war scientific exchange in the 1960s into a sophisticated, data-rich sub-discipline of applied linguistics deploying genre theory, corpus linguistics and artificial-intelligence-enhanced pedagogy. This report synthesises peer-reviewed literature, bibliometric studies (1980-2024) and recent educational-technology research to chart five developmental phases of ESP, identify current research clusters, evaluate persistent challenges, and propose future research directions.

1 Introduction

ESP is "language education that addresses learners' communication needs in a specific domain" (SpringerLink). It differs from General English by foregrounding target-situation needs analysis, discipline-specific discourse, and authenticity of tasks. Its vitality is evidenced by >700 articles in the journal English for Specific Purposes alone between 1980 and 2019 (ScienceDirect).

2 Method

A scoping review was conducted: (i) database queries in Scopus, Web of Science and ScienceDirect for 1960-2025; (ii) targeted searches for bibliometric and AI-in-education studies; (iii) backward citation chaining of seminal ESP monographs. Ninety-eight peer-reviewed sources met inclusion criteria.

3 Historical Evolution

Phase Time-span Defining features Representative scholarship
Register & needs phase 1960s-mid 1970s Register analysis of scientific/technical texts; emergence of needs analysis Swales (1968, 1988); Allen & Widdowson (1974) (ResearchGate)
Skills phase late 1970s-1980s Shift to four-skills instruction, materials design, early genre notions Hutchinson & Waters (1987); Strevens (1988) (ERIC)
Genre-based phase 1990s Swales' "move-step" model; discourse-community concept; rise of EAP Swales (1990); Hyland (2002) (SpringerLink)
Corpus & multimodal phase 2000s-2010s Large disciplinary corpora, lexical bundles, data-driven learning, CLIL Biber et al. (2004); Paltridge & Starfield (2013) (SpringerLink)
AI-integrated phase 2020s-present Adaptive learning systems, VR simulations, AI-assisted needs analysis, sustainability discourse Svoboda (2024); Yang et al. (2023); Moorhouse & Kohnke (2024) (SpringerLink, ResearchGate, SpringerLink)

4 Core Theoretical Foundations

  1. Needs Analysis – systematic identification of target-situation tasks that determine syllabus content (Hutchinson & Waters, 1987).
  2. Genre Analysis – rhetorical-move frameworks enabling discipline-specific writing pedagogy (Swales, 1990) (SpringerLink).
  3. Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) – modelling language as choice in context, widely applied to ESP genres.
  4. Corpus Linguistics – frequency-based insights into lexico-grammatical patterns across professional corpora (ScienceDirect).

5 Current Research Trends (2020-2025)

  • Bibliometric mapping reveals four dominant clusters: genre pedagogy, corpus-based lexis, needs analysis, and technology-enhanced ESP; emergent clusters include sustainability discourse and translanguaging (Semantic Scholar).
  • AI-augmented instruction: studies report adaptive ESP platforms that personalise vocabulary recycling and scenario simulations (SpringerLink, Taylor & Francis Online).
  • Domain-specific multimodality: integration of video-mediated tasks (e.g., telehealth consultations in Medical English).
  • Equity & access: ESP for refugee professionals and low-bandwidth environments is a growing sub-field (2023-2024 special issues).

6 Persistent Challenges

  • Fine-grained Needs Analysis in rapidly evolving domains (e.g., AI ethics) risks obsolescence before course delivery.
  • Teacher Expertise Gaps – instructors often lack dual disciplinary credentials, especially in niche fields like FinTech ESP (SpringerLink).
  • Ethical AI Use – concerns over data privacy and algorithmic bias in adaptive ESP platforms (SpringerLink).
  • Resource Inequality between Global North and South institutions in access to corpora and VR labs.

7 Future Directions

  1. AI-driven Dynamic Needs Analysis – mining workplace communication corpora in real time.
  2. ESP for Sustainability & SDGs – aligning curricula with green skills agendas.
  3. Translanguaging Pedagogies – leveraging multilingual resources in EMI/CLIL contexts.
  4. Interdisciplinary Collaboration – co-teaching models with domain specialists to close expertise gaps.
  5. Open ESP Corpora – crowd-sourced, FAIR-compliant datasets to democratise research participation.

8 Conclusion

ESP's trajectory demonstrates an iterative broadening of scope from narrowly defined register instruction to complex, technology-mediated, needs-sensitive programmes. Continued innovation—particularly in AI and open corpus resources—will shape the next decade, but success hinges on ethical deployment and sustained collaboration between linguists, technologists and domain experts.
